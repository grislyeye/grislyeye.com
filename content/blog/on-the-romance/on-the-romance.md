The history between the OSR and romantic fantasy roleplaying is a tortured one. From the horror of the *Dragonlance Series* of adventures, to the crusade against the original *Blue Rose* roleplaying game, the play styles have often been cast in opposition. I think this is for many reasons (not all of them pleasant) but nominally so because romantic fantasy RPGs are heavily associated with railroading, and synonymous with the much maligned trad play style.

Like a good little GM, I've followed the Discourse and kept my games focussed on maintaining player agency, yet something isn't quite right. When I talk to my players, they aren't particularly concerned about their agency. In fact, they feel railroading actually serves a useful purpose, providing a much-needed push for their characters. And they're not concerned with their choices so much as playing out the interpersonal consequences of those choices.

As one of my players remarked:

> I think the main thing you can do there as a GM is give us those opportunities for our characters to make big choices that they might have different perspectives on, and then give us space to show those interpersonal consequences playing out - which tbh you’re pretty good at imo.

"Interpersonal consequences" is the important phrase here because, for my players, the game seems to really begins when the characters have a quiet moment between action to speak with each other, trying to understand how everyone is feeling and playing out the conflicts their strong emotions create.

Because romantic fantasy is about emotions, and where play is about significant relationships and emotional attachments (i.e. romance) between characters, rather than satisfying narratives plots which are incidental to the enjoyment, rather than core as [others would have it](https://retiredadventurer.blogspot.com/2021/04/six-cultures-of-play.html). It's also the play style that is attracting the most new players, what with *Critical Roles* every growing popularity, heavy railroading and focus on interpersonal relationships between protagonists.

I think back to those *Dragonlance* adventures and wonder if the appeal wasn't the action so much as those moments between the action with everyone sitting around a campfire playing out their emotional responses to the day's events, very much like the characters in the novels. I wonder how many players enjoyed the pauses between vignettes in these adventures, where they could play out the puzzle of engaging with each other's character's needs, drives and motivations. Maybe our thought leaders are too focussed on material agency, and can't see the emotional sandbox romantic fantasy and the trad play style offers.
