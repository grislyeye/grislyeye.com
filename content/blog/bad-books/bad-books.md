Life's too short for bad books. A long time ago I realised that reading for leisure is supposed to be an enjoyable pastime and not a punitive chore. You might feel obliged to finish reading a book even if you don't enjoy it. It's an easy trap to fall into, but there's only so many books you can fit into the rapidly vanishing seconds of your time on earth.

At its core my strategy is pretty straightforward:

>  **Only read books that have at least a four star rating on Goodreads.**

It's that simple.

Why Goodreads? Because book criticism is dead and the only way to get a semi-reliable review is through the network effects of a large sample size of book ratings. And the only semi-reliable source for that information is, unfortunately, Goodreads. Unfortunate because Goodreads is, of course, owned by Amazon. Boo.

Why four stars? Well, I've noticed that reviews lean heavily towards the positive, which shifts the scale. On Goodreads at least, a three star rating is really a two star, and a four star book is really a three star. At least.

Of course, there are caveats - which I'll go into below - but in my experience any book with a lower rating should be dismissed out of hand.

Now, I can hear your objections to this system and I totally agree. I've read plenty of poorly-rated books that I absolutely loved, and even more top-rated books that were just awful. It's not supposed to be a perfect system, it's supposed to narrow the field and reduce the number of bad books I read. I'll ignore a rating and pick up books based on personal recommendations. Authors I know well also get to skip the queue, so to speak. Basically, use your judgement.

Now, the caveats. Firstly, you need to do more than just look at the rating. You also need to ask yourself "who is reviewing this book?" You need a large "sample size" of ratings (usually 1000+) to trust an overall score. Any less than that and ratings are more susceptible to bias.

It's trite to say book reviews are subjective, but it's true in more ways than one. Fans of (not picking on any genre in particular) YA are, obviously, more likely to read, review and enjoy YA fiction. YA is less a genre and more a category of fiction. You can have YA fantasy, thrillers, etc. so you don't just want to look at books that are in your favourite genre. You also want to avoid books that are also in genres you don't like. A book in the YA and scifi category will be very different from a book in the scifi and hard scifi genres. Both might be highly rated, but for different reasons.

You also need to have a working understanding of the genre. It's no good just diving into, to use a personal example, horror and just pick up the first four star review. I know fantasy and science fiction well so I already know what to expect from certain books. On the other hand, genres I'm new to are, so I have discovered, harder to curate.

There is one final trap here: bias. Online ratings and reviews are notoriously unreliable and easy to game. Large numbers of ratings are harder to fake but it's not impossible to sway them. And a dangerous source of bias is review bombing. Everything is political, but reviews more-so now that they have become battlegrounds and useful tools of right-wing propoganda. Is this book really bad or is it just a victim of a hate campaign? This sort of thing happens all the time in film and gaming, and we've seen it happen with books, such as with the right-wing [Sad Puppies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sad_Puppies) campaign to sway Hugo Awards. It's always worth looking at the context within which a book is published (or any piece of culture) and try to understand the political climate surrounding it.

Curating books is a five-dimensional puzzle (literally: 1. rating, 2. review count, 3. genre, 4. personal preference and 5. political climate) and the above only partly solves the problem.
