> “I’ve never really understood ‘world building’ and it seems to derive from D&D etc. about which I know almost nothing.”

 - Michael Moorcock, SF Signal Magazine

I’ve created and adapted my own RPGs in the past but I’ve come to the realisation that game design, while fun, is not what I’m into. What I truly love is worldbuilding. Creating a believable setting and dropping my players into it.

And, thinking on the subject, I’ve considered how you might build worlds badly, and how you might build them well. And to achieve this I think you need to separate worldbuilding into three parts: old-school worldbuilding, world design and world game design.

## Old-School World Building

> “Worldbuilding is dull. Worldbuilding literalises the urge to invent. Worldbuilding gives an unnecessary permission for acts of writing (indeed, for acts of reading). Worldbuilding numbs the reader’s ability to fulfil their part of the bargain, because it believes that it has to do everything around here if anything is going to get done.”

- M John Harrison

World building is the much maligned, much disavowed practice of loading your setting with lore. The guilty pleasure of the Dungeon Master.

There are various popular alternatives to world building: three-hex campaign starters, lazy DM prep, Gygaxian determinism, adventure-as-setting-intro etc.. The idea is all the same, though: the game world evolves bottom-up, as-needed. You create a starting location and place a few, tantalising locations nearby. Then set your players free to explore.

I call these “microsettings”, for want of a popular term.

My position on world building has evolved. I used to find world building

But having run various microsettings, they are fast and put you in the middle of the action. Yet, as your campaign runs beyond the first couple of adventures, they begin to lag. Immersion relies on verisimilitude. Verisimilitude relies on *detail*.

Players start to ask questions that you, as GM, have no prompt answer for. The official guidance is that you improvise details. Or roll for them. But the damage has been done already. The pause signals an “edge of the map” vibe, an emptiness beyond the established setting. The answer frequently rings a bit hollow. Maybe it’s unsympathetic to the existing setting. A jarring change, such as a desert at the edge of a temperate wood. 

World building gives a setting depth, a sense there is a wider world out there. A hook by which a GM might expound and expand on.

There are other aspects of world building that benefit play that I won’t cover in detail here. Aesthetics, consistency, flavour, tone, and so on.

## World Design

> “There is history and geography as well as mechanics, monsters, and items. However, constructing narrative has been given the spotlight here. Random encounters don’t spell out exactly what will happen but rather, are meant to seed a scenario…”

\- *Narfell Setting Guide*

(If you want to understand what I mean by world design, check out the book I’ve quoted above, available from the [DM’s Guild](https://www.dmsguild.com/product/380989/Narfell?affiliate_id=1765521).)

World building gives you a world to explore. However, this is separate to what I like to call world design. World design is what makes a setting playable and enjoyable.

A painful example of an unplayable setting is an empty void. It’s a setting of some kind, but there’s nothing to do.

An unenjoyable setting might be a highly derivative fantasy world, described by dry page and page of uninspiring historical, political and geographical details. The players probably don’t *want* to explore it.

Drilling into this, there are finer considerations. For example, world building for fiction and world building for RPGs is fundamentally different. Fiction world design tends to be more focussed and linear. RPG world design tends to be more sandboxy. GMs need space to to explore, material to create adventures from.

To summarise: world design is a constraint on world building. Necessary, often compromising. Fundamentally, my proposition here is that good world building is about balancing building and design.

## World Game Design

An important aspect of world design needs to be highlighted: does a setting work with your chosen RPG system. A world might be incompatible with your game. For example, a low fantasy setting might not work with D&D.

You might need to do some game design to adapt Core archetypes of your setting might need custom sub-classes, classes. Flavours and tones might need new mechanics. They’ll probably need new monsters. And so forth.
